# Glossary

**antiphony**
:   Music arranged so that two groups of sound answer each other across a distance or across a pause. In musique concrète, antiphony can be created by alternating material between two Simpliciter modules, or between the left and right channels of a stereo recording.

**atonal**
:   Not organised around a tonal centre or key. A piece that moves between pitches without establishing any one pitch as "home". Much musique concrète is effectively atonal, because its pitch content often comes from untreated environmental sounds rather than deliberate tuning.

**attack**
:   The beginning portion of a sound — specifically, how quickly it rises from silence to its full volume. A sharp attack (a handclap, a snare hit) arrives almost instantaneously. A slow attack (a bowed string starting softly) rises gradually. Reversing a sound swaps the attack and decay, producing slow attacks from sounds that originally had sharp ones.

**audio interface**
:   A hardware device that connects microphones and instruments to a computer, converting analogue audio signals to digital data and back. Better than a built-in soundcard for recording quality and for monitoring without latency. Connected to the computer via USB.

**channel**
:   A signal path in a mixer or audio system. A stereo recording has two channels: left and right. Adding more channels allows more simultaneous signals to be controlled independently.

**crescendo**
:   A gradual increase in volume. In VCV Rack, a crescendo can be created by slowly raising a VCA's CV input using an LFO or an envelope generator.

**CV (Control Voltage)**
:   In VCV Rack, a signal used to control module parameters rather than to carry audio. A CV signal might control the playback speed of a Simpliciter, the volume of a VCA, or the cutoff frequency of a filter. CV signals are patched using the same cables as audio signals.

**DAW**
:   Digital Audio Workstation. Software for recording, editing, and mixing audio. Examples include Audacity (free), Reaper (low-cost), Ableton Live, and Logic Pro. VCV Rack is not a DAW — it is a modular synthesiser — but the two can work together.

**decay**
:   The portion of a sound after its peak — how it fades away. A bell has a long, gradual decay. A staccato piano note has a short one. In electronic contexts, "decay" also refers to the second stage of an envelope (Attack-Decay-Sustain-Release, or ADSR).

**diminuendo**
:   A gradual decrease in volume. The opposite of crescendo.

**edit**
:   In tape music, a physical cut of the tape, joining two sections together. In VCV Rack, the equivalent is setting start and end points on a Simpliciter recording, or arranging separate Simpliciter recordings in sequence.

**electronic music**
:   Music produced by electronic means — originally, sounds generated by oscillators, amplifiers, and filters (as opposed to musique concrète, which used recorded concrete sounds as its material). The distinction has largely dissolved: most contemporary electronic music combines both approaches freely.

**Eurorack**
:   The dominant hardware modular synthesiser format, introduced by Dieter Doepfer in 1996. Defines physical dimensions, power supply voltages, and signal standards for hardware modules. VCV Rack is its software equivalent, following many of the same signal conventions including the V/Oct pitch standard.

**flag**
:   Dwyer's term for a marker on a tape timeline. In VCV Rack, Simpliciter's loop start and end points serve the same function — they mark specific locations within a recording.

**floodsound**
:   Dwyer's term for a continuous, undifferentiated background sound that fills a texture. Rain, crowd noise, traffic, and fan noise are floodsounds. They are most useful as textural beds against which more differentiated sounds are placed.

**gate**
:   A signal in VCV Rack that is either fully on (high voltage) or fully off (zero voltage). Used to trigger events: a gate sent to Simpliciter's PLAY input starts playback when the gate goes high. Gate signals can be generated by sequencers, buttons, LFOs configured as square waves, or other modules.

**granular synthesis**
:   A technique derived from tape splicing: sound is cut into tiny fragments called grains (typically 1–100 milliseconds) and these grains are rearranged, overlapped, pitched, and scattered. The result can range from subtle stretching and thickening of a sound to clouds of texture that have little audible relationship to the original. The Audible Instruments Texture Synthesizer module in VCV Rack is a granular processor.

**head**
:   In a tape recorder, the physical component that reads or writes magnetic information on the tape. The playback head reads; the record head writes; the erase head removes previous recordings. In VCV Rack, these functions are handled invisibly inside the Simpliciter module.

**hi-fi**
:   High-fidelity. Reproduction that is accurate to the original sound, with low noise and minimal colouration. Historically associated with the audiophile movement of the 1950s–70s.

**LFO (Low Frequency Oscillator)**
:   An oscillator running below the audible frequency range — typically below 20 Hz. Because its oscillation is too slow to hear as a pitch, it is used to modulate other parameters over time: a sine-wave LFO connected to a VCA creates a slow volume pulse; connected to a Simpliciter's SPEED input, it creates periodic pitch variation.

**line**
:   A signal at line level — the standard signal strength used between audio devices (mixers, tape machines, effects units). Distinguished from microphone level (lower) and speaker level (higher). In VCV Rack, all signals are normalised and the concept of line level is handled automatically.

**loop**
:   A segment of audio that repeats continuously. In tape music, loops were created by physically splicing a length of tape into a circle. In Simpliciter, you set loop start and end points and enable looping; the module returns to the start point when it reaches the end.

**machine**
:   Dwyer's general term for the tape recorder. In this book, the machine is the Simpliciter module.

**mike**
:   Microphone. The device that converts sound pressure waves into an electrical signal. The starting point for all musique concrète.

**mirror structure**
:   A compositional form in which material is presented and then reflected: A-B-C-B-A, or a sequence that runs forward and then backward. Mirror structures can apply to the large-scale sections of a piece or to small-scale patterns within a section.

**Mistone**
:   Dwyer's term for sounds produced by musical instruments in unconventional ways: extended techniques, accidental sounds, deliberate abuse. A violin bow applied near the bridge with excessive pressure; piano strings plucked or struck directly; keys clicked without blowing. Mistones retain the timbral associations of their instrument while shedding conventional pitch behaviour.

**modular synthesiser**
:   A synthesiser built from interchangeable modules — oscillators, filters, amplifiers, envelopes, sequencers — connected by patch cables. The signal path is determined by the patching, not by a fixed internal design. VCV Rack is a software modular synthesiser; Eurorack is its hardware counterpart.

**module**
:   A single functional unit in VCV Rack: an oscillator, a filter, a tape recorder (Simpliciter), a mixer, a sequencer, an effects processor. Modules communicate by sending and receiving signals through their input and output jacks.

**mono**
:   Single-channel audio. A mono recording has one channel; it sounds the same through left and right speakers. Contrast with stereo.

**monitor**
:   A loudspeaker used for accurate audio reproduction in a recording or mixing context. Also, the act of listening to a signal as it is recorded or processed.

**motive**
:   A short, recurring musical idea — a rhythmic pattern, a characteristic interval, a specific sound object that appears at different points in a piece and gives it unity. In musique concrète, a motive might be a specific recorded sound, or a specific rhythmic relationship between two sounds.

**musique concrète**
:   Music made from recorded, manipulated sounds — "concrete" sounds from the real world, as opposed to abstract synthesised tones. Coined by Pierre Schaeffer in Paris in 1948. The founding practice of composed electronic music.

**noise**
:   A sound perceived as disorganised, unwanted, or intrusive. Crucially, noise is a judgement, not a property of the sound itself. In musique concrète, sounds that would be considered noise in other contexts are deliberately treated as compositional material.

**ostinato**
:   A rhythmic or melodic pattern that repeats persistently throughout a piece, or a section of one. Loops in musique concrète naturally produce ostinati. An ostinato provides rhythmic stability and can be used as a foundation against which less regular material plays.

**patch**
:   A VCV Rack configuration: the complete set of modules and their cable connections, saved as a `.vcv` file. The word comes from the patch cables of hardware modular synthesis.

**patch cable**
:   A virtual cable connecting one module's output to another's input in VCV Rack. Dragged from output jack to input jack with the mouse. Different colours can be assigned to help organise complex patches.

**Pseudotone**
:   Dwyer's term for environmental sounds that have a musical or quasi-musical pitch character without being produced by musical instruments. A humming transformer, a spinning fan, a wine glass tapped with a spoon, a kettle coming to the boil. Pseudotones are among the most musically flexible sounds in musique concrète.

**recorder**
:   Any device that captures and stores sound for playback. In Dwyer's book, the reel-to-reel tape recorder. In this book, the NYSTHI Simpliciter module.

**register**
:   The range of pitch in which a sound falls — high register, middle register, low register. Changing the playback speed of a Simpliciter recording shifts the register of the recorded material.

**reverb**
:   Short for reverberation. The persistence of sound in a space after the original source has stopped, as reflections from walls, ceiling, and floor continue to arrive at the listener. Natural reverb gives a sense of space; heavy reverb blurs and washes sounds together.

**sample rate**
:   The number of audio samples recorded or played back per second. The standard for CD-quality audio is 44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz): 44,100 measurements of air pressure per second. Higher sample rates (96 kHz, 192 kHz) capture more high-frequency detail but require more processing and storage. For the work in this book, 44,100 Hz is sufficient.

**saturation**
:   The soft clipping effect of overdriven magnetic tape: when the signal exceeds the tape's capacity, the peaks are gently rounded rather than harshly clipped, producing warmth, compression, and harmonic enrichment. Tape saturation is widely emulated in software; the NYSTHI Console module in VCV Rack provides this quality.

**serial**
:   A compositional method — associated with Schoenberg, Berg, and Webern — in which a sequence (series) of pitches, rhythms, or other elements is used as the structural basis for a piece, each element appearing in a predetermined order. Serial technique is rarely used in musique concrète, but the idea of ordering material according to a predetermined scheme has analogs in sequencer-based VCV Rack composition.

**signal**
:   An electrical representation of audio or control information, flowing through cables from one module to another.

**Simpliciter**
:   The NYSTHI virtual tape recorder module for VCV Rack. Records audio, plays it back at any speed and in any direction, supports looping and overdubbing, and accepts CV inputs for playback speed and other parameters. The central instrument of this book.

**Sone**
:   Dwyer's term for sounds with no discernible pitch and no musical instrument associations — pure texture, pure noise in the neutral sense. Footsteps, rain, traffic, crowd noise, breaking glass, tearing paper. Sones form the rhythmic and textural backbone of much musique concrète.

**sound**
:   The sensation produced when vibrations reach the ear. In this book, any sound is potential musical material.

**speed**
:   In Simpliciter, the rate at which a recording is played back relative to its original recorded speed. Speed below 1.0 slows the playback (pitch drops); speed above 1.0 raises it. Speed can be set manually by dragging the SPEED knob or controlled by a CV signal.

**stereo**
:   Two-channel audio — left and right — designed to create a sense of space and width. Sounds can be positioned anywhere between the two speakers using panning. Contrast with mono.

**superimposition**
:   Layering two or more recordings simultaneously — playing them at the same time so that they combine into a single texture. The equivalent of mixing two tracks together. In VCV Rack, superimposition is achieved by mixing the outputs of two or more Simpliciter modules.

**tape music**
:   Music composed using magnetic tape as the primary medium — recording, editing, and transforming sounds physically. The direct predecessor of all electronic music composition. Techniques developed for tape (loop, splice, reversal, speed change) map directly onto VCV Rack's Simpliciter module.

**tempo**
:   The speed of musical pulse or movement in a piece. In musique concrète, tempo is often implicit in rhythmic patterns of attacks rather than in a regular beat. A gate sequencer running at a fixed clock speed establishes an explicit tempo.

**ternary**
:   Three-part form: A-B-A. A musical structure that establishes material, departs from it, and returns. One of the most fundamental large-scale forms in Western music.

**texture**
:   The overall surface quality of a musical moment — dense or sparse, smooth or rough, stable or turbulent, high or low. Texture is determined by the number of simultaneous sounds, their register, their attack characteristics, and their rhythmic behaviour.

**timbre**
:   The characteristic quality of a sound that makes it recognisable — the colour that distinguishes a clarinet from a flute playing the same note. In musique concrète, transformed sounds often retain their timbral identity even when pitch and duration are radically altered. A reversed bell still sounds glassy.

**tonality**
:   The organisation of musical material around a tonal centre — a "home" pitch or key. Much Western art music from 1600–1900 is tonal. Musique concrète is generally non-tonal, though it may use tonal material as raw sound.

**Tone**
:   Dwyer's term for sounds produced by musical instruments in the conventional sense: sounds with clear, definite pitch, produced intentionally for musical purposes.

**tone-colour**
:   Another word for timbre. The quality that distinguishes one instrument or voice from another at the same pitch and volume.

**tremolo**
:   Regular, rapid variation in volume. Produced naturally by some instruments (the wavering of a sustained accordion note) and artificially by effects. In VCV Rack, a tremolo effect can be created by connecting a fast LFO to a VCA's CV input.

**VCA (Voltage Controlled Amplifier)**
:   A module that controls the volume of an audio signal using a CV input. When the CV is high, the signal passes at full volume; when low, it is attenuated or silenced. VCAs are the fundamental tool for dynamic control in modular synthesis.

**VCV Rack**
:   Free, open-source software modular synthesiser platform, first released in 2017 by Andrew Belt. Available for Windows, Mac, and Linux from vcvrack.com. The central software tool of this book.

**vibrato**
:   Regular, rapid variation in pitch. Produced by singers and string players as an expressive technique. In VCV Rack, vibrato can be applied to Simpliciter playback by connecting a fast LFO to the SPEED input.

**V/Oct (Volts per Octave)**
:   The pitch standard used in Eurorack hardware and VCV Rack: one volt of CV corresponds to one octave of pitch change. Two volts: two octaves. This standard allows CV signals from sequencers and keyboards to control pitch in a predictable, musical way.

**wow**
:   The slow, cyclical pitch variation caused by uneven tape transport speed — the tape moving slightly faster or slower than it should. Wow produces a characteristic seasick quality associated with ageing or failing tape machines. It can be simulated in VCV Rack by connecting a very slow LFO to Simpliciter's SPEED input with a small modulation depth.
