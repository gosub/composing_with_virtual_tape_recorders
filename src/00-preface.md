# Preface

In 1971, a British music educator named Terence Dwyer published a slim, cheerful book called *Composing with Tape Recorders: Musique Concrète for Beginners*. It was addressed to schoolchildren, hobbyists, and curious adults who wanted to make music but didn't know where to start. Dwyer's central argument was disarmingly simple: you don't need conservatory training, you don't need expensive instruments, and you don't need permission. You need a tape recorder, a microphone, and the willingness to listen carefully to the world around you.

That book changed how a lot of people thought about music-making. It also shaped, directly or indirectly, much of what we now call electronic music culture. And yet it was written for a technology that most people no longer own. Reel-to-reel tape recorders are antiques. The splicing blocks and razor blades Dwyer describes with such enthusiasm are museum pieces — though [Hainbach](https://www.youtube.com/@Hainbach) has made a devoted art form of rescuing them from exactly those museums.

This book is an attempt to honour Dwyer's spirit while replacing his tools with ones you can actually get hold of today, for free, on any computer.

The physical tape recorder becomes **VCV Rack**, a free, open-source modular software synthesizer that runs on Windows, Mac, and Linux. You can download it from [vcvrack.com](https://vcvrack.com) at no cost. The particular module that stands in for Dwyer's tape machine is the **NYSTHI Simpliciter**, a virtual sampler that records sounds, plays them back, and lets you manipulate them in ways that map almost exactly onto the techniques Dwyer describes. The NYSTHI plugin collection, which includes Simpliciter, is also free, available through the VCV Library with a few clicks.

That's the whole apparatus. A computer you probably already own, two free software downloads, and a microphone (even a built-in laptop microphone will get you started). Dwyer would have approved.

Every technique in the original book has a direct equivalent in Simpliciter: all twelve lettered methods, A through L, covering speed changes, direction reversal, tape loops, layering, and more. Where Dwyer turns a reel at half speed, you drag a knob. Where he splices tape to create a loop, you set loop points with a mouse click. The physics of magnetic tape and the logic of digital sampling are different at a deep level, but at the level of *what you can do with sound*, they are remarkably similar. Musique concrète is, at its heart, a way of thinking about recorded sound as raw material, and that idea is entirely independent of the medium.

One place where this book diverges from Dwyer's is in its treatment of effects. Dwyer had access to spring reverbs, echo units, and the natural saturation of tape. VCV Rack gives you access to thousands of effects modules: reverb, delay, tape saturation emulation, pitch shifting, granular processing, and stranger things besides. These are genuinely exciting, but they can also be overwhelming for a beginner. So this book handles them the same way a good recipe book handles optional garnishes: they appear in **Creative Option** boxes, clearly marked, separate from the main instructions. Nothing in a Creative Option box is required. You can ignore every one of them and still complete every exercise. When you're ready to explore, they'll be there.

The structure of the chapters follows Dwyer's original closely. His philosophy, that self-criticism matters more than external validation, that the work of making is inseparable from the work of listening, that anyone can do this, is reproduced here as faithfully as I can manage, because it hasn't aged a day. (A note on that word *self-criticism*: in today's creative culture it can sound like a recipe for paralysis. Dwyer meant something more like *curiosity plus taste*: the habit of listening back to what you made and asking honestly whether it surprised you.)

Thank you, Terence Dwyer, for writing a book generous enough to be worth adapting fifty years later.

Now: open VCV Rack, load a Simpliciter, point a microphone at something interesting, and press record. The rest will follow.
